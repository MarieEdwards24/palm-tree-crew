Brand Director Case Study

Palm Tree Crew

Marie Edwards  ·  2026

"The cultural moments are already there. The architecture makes the impossible inevitable."

Opening

PTC is the most compelling lifestyle brand opportunity since Margaritaville.

  • Margaritaville proved a feeling — not a product, not a category — could become a billion-dollar ecosystem
  • Palm Tree Crew has done the hardest part: created authentic cultural demand that cannot be manufactured
  • The community has opted in, the gravitational force is real
  • I'm the person who builds the foundation so the next impossible idea lands somewhere actionable and compounds
Brand Diagnosis

PTC is Margaritaville for a new generation — same architecture, better raw material.

The Proof of Concept
  • Jimmy Buffett turned a single feeling into hotels, restaurants, a SiriusXM channel, retirement communities, and a tequila brand
  • All of it sustained by a community who recognized themselves in it and kept showing up for decades
  • Margaritaville is the proof of concept. PTC is the evolved version.
Why PTC Has Better Raw Material
  • Audience is younger, more globally mobile, more financially sophisticated, and more identity-driven
  • PTC's community are builders, investors, tastemakers, and cultural operators — not just fans
  • That overlap of creative ambition and financial fluency in one room is not accidental
What Cannot Be Manufactured
  • Authentic cultural demand — the thing every brand tries to buy and almost none earn
  • The goal: make it commercially available 365 days a year, across every category the community inhabits
  • Independent of any single event or any single person
Brand Diagnosis

The foundation is extraordinary. The infrastructure to hold it needs to be, too.

Strongest Assets
  • Live event product: world-class — Aspen, Hamptons, Hawaii, Art Basel, St. Barths, Grand Prix
  • Founders' networks open doors no marketing budget can
  • Network density: builders, investors, tastemakers in one room — Margaritaville never had this
  • Access is not brand equity — it's the condition that makes brand building possible
Critical Gaps
  • The Crew is the brand's most powerful idea and it has no infrastructure
  • Community without infrastructure is just an audience. Audiences are rented. Communities are owned.
  • The content gap: brand goes quiet between events — every week of silence is missed momentum
  • In person: world-class. Online: any other festival brand.
What It Should Evolve Toward
  • Architecture strong enough that the brand amplifies the founders rather than depending on them
  • Personality attracts the community — architecture is what lets the community find its home when the personality isn't in the room
First 90 Days

Everything in this presentation is built from public signals. That's the starting point, not the plan.

  • I haven't been inside the organization — and the most dangerous move is executing against assumptions rather than reality
  • The honest answer to the first 90 days: spend them finding out what's actually true
  • The brand document I'll build won't just guide the work — it will free the people doing it
  • When everyone has the filter, the micro-decisions stop being escalated and the work gets bolder, more confident, and more exponential
First 90 Days

Listen. Name. Amplify. Build. In that order.

01
Listen Before Naming
  • Talk to everyone: internal team, partners, venue operators, long-time community members, and people who came once and never came back
  • What does PTC feel like at its best? What feels off? What do people say when we're not in the room?
  • Social listening simultaneously — what does the community say when they don't think the brand is watching?
  • One output only: the truth about what the brand already is
02
Build the Brand Document
  • The decision lever — not guidelines in a folder, but the filter every subsequent decision runs through
  • Content, partnerships, product, licensing, hiring — does this belong in the PTC world or doesn't it?
  • Built with the team, not for them — co-authors create ownership, ownership creates confidence
  • Internal trust, autonomy, and clarity — the team works with excitement instead of hesitation
03
Find the Closest Alligator to the Boat
  • Look at what's already in motion — which existing event, space, or moment can the brand amplify most fully right now?
  • First proof the brand document works as a decision lever
  • Builds internal credibility fast — the team sees brand work isn't slow and invisible
04
The Next Step That Earns Its Place
  • Honest answer: I don't know yet — it emerges from what Phases 1–3 reveal
  • Option A: Community architecture pilot, Miami + New York, padel-anchored
  • Option B: Content operation, always-on editorial presence holding the red thread between events
  • The evidence decides — not this presentation

Global Brand Narrative

The Crew shows up to build something, not just experience something. They're not passive. They're participants.

"The Crew doesn't consume culture. They make it."

The Unifying Idea
  • Every great lifestyle brand is built on a single idea so specific it naturally excludes the wrong people and magnetically attracts the right ones
  • It promises participation, not just access
  • It opens the full category expansion: a community building culture needs places, products, venues, trips, and investments
  • Emotional territory: active arrival — not escapism, showing up somewhere that matters and leaving more connected than when you arrived
Pillar 01
Belonging

The foundation. Cannot be manufactured by spending money, only earned through consistency, curation, and genuine investment. Every touchpoint deepens it or dilutes it.

Pillar 02

Social Media & Visual Presence

Event marketing, not world building. Beautiful posts, no portable brand signature.

The Diagnosis
  • Looked at the feed the way a potential member would — someone who hasn't been to a festival, spending two minutes deciding if this world is for them
  • Photography is beautiful, production value is real — none of that is the problem
  • The feed announces, recaps, and promotes — nothing makes the brand's world feel real on a Thursday in January with no event coming
  • Brand goes quiet between events when it should be amplifying
01
Stop Posting Events. Start Building the World.
  • A content framework that runs independently of the event calendar — three recurring territories every single week
  • Day 1: profile someone in the Crew, not because they're famous, but because they're interesting and building something
  • Day 4: capture the specific visual texture of the PTC world — a detail, a place, a moment
  • Day 7: connect PTC to a cultural moment or partnership that answers the "why"
  • Impact: 365-day presence, better audience quality, brand stops renting attention and starts owning it
Social Media & Visual Presence

Find the visual feeling. Give the audience somewhere to go.

02
Visual Feeling, Not Visual System
  • The goal is not a recognizable grid — a grid too coherent becomes predictable, and predictable is unforgivable for this community
  • Study the brands whose feeds stop people mid-scroll — not festival brands, but brands whose posts create genuine desire
  • Show what happens before the stage is built, the artist nobody knows yet, the 20 minutes after when people are still feeling it
  • People love three things: behind the scenes, finding out first, feeling part of a conversation that matters — we have all three
  • Visual identity follows the content — when the content is alive enough, coherence emerges naturally
03
Give the Audience an Audience
  • Right now it's broadcast — the brand posts, the audience watches, no participation is invited
  • The brand starts talking back — every meaningful comment gets a real response in the brand's voice
  • Participatory moments — genuine invitations into the world, not engagement bait
  • Every genuine response moves someone one step closer to being part of the Crew

"I want to be part of whatever that is."

Palm Tree Cultural Moment

Grammy Week, LA. February. Annual. PTC × Belmond. The most specific invitation of music's most important week.

The Gap
  • No premium lifestyle brand owns Grammy week — and PTC is co-founded by a Grammy-nominated artist
  • Every major label hosts dinners, every streaming platform hosts parties — none operate at the intersection of music, culture, entrepreneurship, and community
  • A music lifestyle brand that doesn't own Grammy week is making an unintentional identity statement
Why "Arrivals"
  • Active arrival — not a party you attend, a place you arrive with intention, with your ambitions, with the people you're building alongside
  • Direct counterpoint to Margaritaville: escape vs. arrival — same warmth and belonging, opposite direction of travel
  • It compounds — Palm Tree Arrivals 2026, 2027, 2028 — each year the name accumulates cultural meaning
The Format
  • Afternoon: cultural programming series in a Belmond-curated space — intimate conversations between builders, not panels
  • Culminates in everyone watching the Grammy ceremony together — belonging crystallizes in that shared moment
  • Evening: performance and dinner — curated, invite-only, no ticket sales — invited because you belong in the room
Brand Discipline

Stop letting the founders' personal brands carry the weight the brand system should carry.

What I'm Naming
  • This names something that is both PTC's greatest asset and its most significant structural vulnerability
  • Right now the founders are doing a job an architecturally sound brand system should be doing
  • I've worked through this same challenge with Marie Kondo and Martha Stewart — the founder's personality was doing work the brand system needed to learn to do on its own
The Margaritaville Lesson
  • When Buffett died, the brand survived because they spent decades building architecture around the feeling he embodied
  • PTC has active, culturally relevant founders — an extraordinary asset, and a window that's open right now
  • Build while the raw material is at its best
The Replacement
  • A brand system so clearly an expression of the founders' taste that it makes their involvement more powerful, not less necessary
  • Built with them, not for them — co-authored so it travels without them being in the room
  • Specific enough that someone who's never met Kygo or Myles can make a brand decision they would have made themselves

"Their personality amplifies the brand. The brand no longer depends on their personality."

Research Reference

Brand deep-dives and strategic context. Not for presentation — for reference.

Loro Piana
The Material Standard

Founded in 1924 in Quarona, Italy, Loro Piana spent a century sourcing the world's rarest natural fibers — vicuña, baby cashmere, Storm System wool — and turning them into garments so extraordinary that the product itself is the entire argument. No celebrity campaigns. No logo saturation. The brand generated an estimated €1.6 billion in revenue in 2024 with EBITDA margins that doubled year over year, achieved almost entirely through quality and word of mouth. CEO Damien Bertrand pushed back on the "quiet luxury" label explicitly: "Loro Piana existed before quiet luxury and will exist afterwards. There are a lot of things about the brand that are not quiet at all." The logo is incidental. The fabric is the identity. If you know, you know. If you don't, you're not the audience.

What PTC Extracts

Premium does not require noise. The brand's quality — the specificity of the event experience, the precision of the curation, the caliber of the people in the room — should be legible without annotation. The right person walks into a PTC event and knows immediately they are somewhere that required serious thought and serious taste.

Highsnobiety
The Editorial Voice

Founded in Berlin in 2005 as a personal passion blog about streetwear culture, Highsnobiety grew into one of the most commercially influential fashion and culture media companies in the world, ultimately acquired by Zalando for its ability to "turn stories into products and products into stories." Their managing editor described their reader as "someone who likes to be informed informally." Their New Luxury research found that 71% of their audience believes the story around a collaboration is just as important as the product itself. Cultural credibility came before commercial credibility — and the commercial credibility followed.

What PTC Extracts

The content infrastructure argument in the $1M allocation is the Highsnobiety model applied to PTC's world. Not a social media operation — an editorial operation with a defined cultural point of view. The distinction is the whole point.

Aman
The Spatial Philosophy

Founded in 1988 when Adrian Zecha built a private retreat on a headland in Phuket too beautiful to keep to himself, Aman introduced a philosophy that has never been successfully replicated: intimacy as the primary luxury. While every other five-star hotel competed to add more rooms and more amenities, Aman limited each property to under 50 rooms, hired staff for genuine care rather than scripted service, and chose locations for cultural and historical significance. The result is the Amanjunkie phenomenon — guests who visit a dozen or more properties, plan annual calendars around new openings, and maintain loyalty with no parallel in the hotel industry. The Aman Club, launched in 2023, has a 92% renewal rate. Repeat guests account for 60% of all bookings.

What PTC Extracts

Every physical decision is a brand decision. The height of a ceiling, the direction a venue faces, the sound of the space when it's quiet — these are not operational details. When PTC builds or licenses a physical space, the spatial philosophy should communicate the world before a single word is spoken.

Acne Studios
The Typographic Identity

Founded in Stockholm in 1996 as something between a creative collective and a fashion experiment, Acne Studios made branding its primary point of difference from the beginning. The iconic millennial pink shopping bag, chosen because "people considered pink to be ugly," became a cultural artifact used by people who had never purchased anything from the brand. The brand avoided traditional advertising for its first two decades, building to $285M in annual revenue through a biannual print magazine featuring David Lynch and Tilda Swinton, stores in off-beat locations, and collaborations chosen for cultural alignment. Net income surged 135% in 2024. When their CEO admitted "most people barely know that we exist," he was describing a deliberate strategy — depth before breadth.

What PTC Extracts

A typographic signature and visual language so specific that people recognize the brand before they read the name is a commercial asset worth building deliberately. PTC needs its equivalent — not a logo, but a visual language so ownable that encountering it in any context tells you immediately what world you're in.

i-D Magazine
The Community Photography

Founded in London in 1980 by Terry Jones as a hand-stapled fanzine, i-D introduced the Straight-Up — a full-length portrait of a street-cast subject accompanied by a brief Q&A about their life. The mechanism: i-D finds people before other publications find them, treats them as already significant, and in doing so helps make them significant. The discovery is the editorial act. The photographers who learned their craft at i-D include Wolfgang Tillmans, Mario Testino, and Nick Knight. The magazine returned in Spring 2025 under Bedford Media and achieved its highest-ever print revenue on the first issue, featuring an 18-year-old discovered through an open casting call.

What PTC Extracts

The way PTC photographs its community should make every subject feel the way i-D makes its subjects feel — seen, celebrated, close. Not polished event photography shot from a distance. The Palm Tree Table chef discovered in Sardinia. The Crew member before the padel match. The i-D frame: this person matters, and we found them first.

The Ned
The Hospitality Architecture

The Ned opened in London in 2017 in the former Midland Bank building — a Grade I listed Edwin Lutyens-designed banking hall. Conceived by Nick Jones (founder of Soho House), it transformed 11 floors into a hotel and members club with 250 bedrooms, 10 restaurants, 17 bars, two pools, and a curated art collection. What is remarkable is not the scale but that every space within the building feels like a natural expression of a single world. The membership (£4,000/year, referral required) operates on the principle that the club exists for "diverse professionals to meet, work and have a good time." Properties have since opened in New York, Washington DC, and Doha.

What PTC Extracts

The Ned is the closest existing reference to what Palm Tree Club Miami should eventually feel like. Not Soho House (too generic through scale), not Aman (prioritizes retreat over social energy). The Ned at its best: different things happening simultaneously — all feeling like the same world. That multi-layered quality is what PTC designs toward in its permanent venues.

Soho House
The Membership Architecture — and the Cautionary Tale

Soho House was founded in London in 1995 by Nick Jones as a private members club for people in the creative industries. The founding thesis: build a room for the right people and let the room do the work. By 2025, Soho House operated 40+ properties globally across multiple membership tiers, a co-working concept, a spa brand, and a product line. The model proved that a community membership brand could expand into hospitality, retail, and lifestyle without losing its identity — as long as the curation standards held. The cautionary tale: Soho House's IPO in 2021 and subsequent financial pressure led to membership expansion that diluted the original curation. The rooms began to feel less like the right people and more like everyone who could afford the annual fee.

What PTC Extracts

The tiered membership architecture is the direct structural model for the Crew. The Soho House failure mode is the specific thing PTC must design protections against from day one. Community membership brands don't die from lack of demand. They die from losing the curation that made the demand real.

Red Bull Media House
Content as Brand Infrastructure

Red Bull's transformation from energy drink to cultural institution is the most studied case study in brand-as-publisher. Red Bull Media House produces Red Bull TV, the Red Bulletin magazine, and content across action sports, music, gaming, and culture — generating more cultural credibility per dollar than any advertising budget could purchase. The brand's genius: laddered pathways from local to global. Community engagement begins with grassroots activations and local competitions, then creates aspirational escalations toward global events. A person who watched a local Red Bull qualifier feels differently about Red Bull Rampage than someone who just saw an ad. That ladder is exactly what PTC is missing.

What PTC Extracts

The 35% content infrastructure investment is the Red Bull Media House model at PTC's current scale. Not sponsor the culture — document it, own it, compound it. The padel community pilot is the first rung of the PTC ladder — connecting the everyday community moment to the headline event with something that makes both more meaningful.

Supreme
The Access Architecture

Supreme was founded in 1994 by James Jebbia on Lafayette Street in Manhattan as a store for New York's skate community. The founding community was hyperlocal and self-selecting — neighborhood kids, skaters, and local artists who became the store's staff and customers before they became its brand advocates. The mechanism Supreme perfected is drop culture: new products released weekly in strictly limited quantities, never restocked. The scarcity is deliberate and structural. The resale market that emerges is not a problem to manage but a free marketing engine. Items selling at a 57% average premium on StockX are evidence that the community values the brand more than the retail price can express. Supreme generated $538M in revenue in fiscal 2024 from just 17 stores globally — an extraordinary revenue-per-door figure that only scarcity and cultural demand can produce. VF Corporation acquired Supreme for $2.1B in 2020 and sold it to EssilorLuxottica for $1.5B in 2024 — the decline reflecting the market's skepticism about whether corporate ownership could preserve the cultural credibility the brand's value depends on.

What PTC Extracts

Supreme's most instructive contribution is the underlying principle — that access architecture is a brand decision. The way PTC structures who gets invited to Palm Tree Arrivals, who gets Crew membership, who receives early access to new markets is not an operational question. It is the most powerful brand statement PTC makes continuously. The community that forms around genuine scarcity and genuine curation is qualitatively different from the one that forms around open access.

Ennismore
The Lifestyle Hospitality Collective

Ennismore was founded in 2011 by Sharan Pasricha before entering a joint venture with Accor in 2021, creating an autonomous entity within the world's sixth-largest hospitality company. Ennismore today operates 16 brands across 184 hotels in 40 countries with 140 properties in the pipeline. The brand architecture organizes around three pillars: the Lifestyle Collective (The Hoxton, Mama Shelter, 25hours Hotels, Mondrian, SLS), Immersive Resorts (Rixos), and Iconic Venues (Paris Society). Ennismore reported 17.6% net unit growth and €170M EBITDA in 2024. Its most strategically interesting innovation is Dis-loyalty — a paid membership program ($18/month) that inverts the conventional hotel loyalty model entirely, rewarding members for going somewhere new rather than returning to the same property. "We don't reward you for how often you stay with us. We reward you for being disloyal." Members book longer stays, further in advance, with a higher average booking value. Most recoup the annual fee in a single stay. Accor began evaluating a potential IPO for Ennismore in late 2024.

What PTC Extracts

Ennismore is the single most directly instructive structural reference for PTC's long-term architecture. Not aesthetically — but because Ennismore solved the same fundamental problem PTC will face at scale: how do you maintain cultural credibility and founder-built identity while expanding globally through an institutional ownership structure? The answer: keep the founders in the room, keep the creative in-house, build the operational infrastructure to scale around the creative core. The Dis-loyalty model is also directly relevant — the hospitality industry's most sophisticated proof of concept for a community membership program that rewards exploration over accumulation.
